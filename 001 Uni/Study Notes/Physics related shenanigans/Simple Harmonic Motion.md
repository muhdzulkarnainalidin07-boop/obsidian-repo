### What is SHM?
Simple Harmonic Motion — any oscillating system where the restoring force is proportional to displacement. Springs, pendulums, tuning forks all do this.

SHM requires F = −kx (force always pulling back toward centre).
### The three big equations
Displacement
x(t) = x$_m$ cos(ωt + φ)
x$_m$ = amplitude, φ = phase constant
Velocity
v(t) = −ωx$_m$ sin(ωt + φ)
Max speed = ωx$_m$
Acceleration
a(t) = −ω²x$_m$ cos(ωt + φ)
Max accel = ω²x_m, also a = −ω²x
Angular frequency
ω = √(k/m) = 2πf
### Energy in SHM
Energy constantly swaps between kinetic and potential. Total mechanical energy stays constant (no friction).
Potential energy
U = ½kx²
Kinetic energy
K = ½mv²
Total mechanical energy
E = ½kx_m²
Constant — depends only on amplitude
At max displacement → all potential, zero kinetic. At equilibrium → all kinetic, zero potential.
### Types of pendulums
Simple pendulum
T = 2π√(L/g)
Only valid for small angles
Physical pendulum
T = 2π√(I/mgL)
I = rotational inertia, L = dist to pivot
Torsion pendulum
T = 2π√(I/κ)
κ = torsion constant
### Damped oscillation
Real oscillators lose energy to friction/air resistance — the amplitude shrinks over time.
Damped displacement
x(t) = x_m e^(−bt/2m) cos(ω't + φ)
b = damping constant. The exponential envelope shrinks the peaks.
Energy decays as E(t) = ½kx_m² · e^(−bt/m)
### Resonance
If you drive a system at its natural frequency (ω_d = ω), the amplitude grows large — this is resonance. The velocity amplitude is maximum at resonance.

Real-world risk: bridges, buildings, wine glasses shattering — all resonance phenomena.
