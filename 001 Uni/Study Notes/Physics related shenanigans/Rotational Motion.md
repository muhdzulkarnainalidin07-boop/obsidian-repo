### Rotation mirrors linear motion — everything has an angular version
| Linear         | Angular              |
| -------------- | -------------------- |
| position x     | angle θ              |
| velocity v     | angular vel ω        |
| acceleration a | angular accel α      |
| mass m         | rotational inertia I |
| force F        | torque τ             |
| F = ma         | τ = Iα               |
| K = ½mv²       | K = ½Iω²             |

### Key relationships: rotation ↔ linear

Arc length
s = rθ

Tangential speed
v = rω

Tangential accel
a$_t$ = rα

Centripetal accel
a$_c$ = v²/r = ω²r

### Rotational inertia (I)

How hard it is to spin something — the rotational version of mass. Depends on _how the mass is distributed_, not just total mass.

Discrete masses
I = Σ m$_i$ r$_i$²

Continuous body
I = ∫ r² dm

Parallel axis theorem
I = I$_c$om + Mh²
==h = distance from centre of mass to new axis==

**Mass far from the axis → harder to spin (higher I). Think of a figure skater pulling arms in to spin faster.**
### Torque
Torque
τ = r × F = rF sin(θ)
r = moment arm (distance from pivot). θ = angle between r and F.

*Torque is the rotational equivalent of force. Newton's 2nd: τ$_n$$_e$$_t$ = Iα*
