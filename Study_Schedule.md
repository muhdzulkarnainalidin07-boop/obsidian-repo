# Study Schedule — Math & Physics
Math exam: Day 5 (daytime). Physics exam: Day 6 (daytime).
Study window: 8pm–midnight (peak hours).
Format: 25 min on / 10 min off. Check pass = last 5 min of each session only.

---

## Ground Rules
- One subject per session. No crossing over.
- Finish the current problem when the timer hits, then stop.
- Check pass window = last 5 minutes only. One pass, then forward.
- If you hyperfocus, finish the problem you're on — then stop anyway.
- Sleep is not optional prep time. Hard stop by midnight every night.

---

## Day 1
*Math: Vectors + Lines & Planes. Physics: Kinematics + Forces.*

### MATH — 8:00pm to 10:10pm

**8:00–8:25 | Vectors: basics and dot product**
Read your Vectors notes for 10 minutes, then close them. Spend the remaining 15 minutes writing out every dot product formula and rule from memory. Don't check until the check pass at 8:20.

*Break 8:25–8:35*

**8:35–9:00 | Vectors: cross product**
Close notes immediately. Reconstruct the cross product determinant expansion from scratch. Write it out 3 times if needed — this one needs motor memory. Check pass at 8:55.

*Break 9:00–9:10*

**9:10–9:35 | Lines & Planes: line forms**
Read the three line forms once, then close. Write all three from memory with a made-up example for each. Feel the difference between them, don't just copy the formula. Check pass at 9:30.

*Break 9:35–9:45*

**9:45–10:10 | Lines & Planes: planes and intersections**
Focus on finding the normal vector from three points — that step trips most people. Write the plane equation, the method for getting n from three points, and all distance/angle formulas from memory. Check pass at 10:05.

---

### PHYSICS — 10:20pm to 11:50pm

**10:20–10:45 | Kinematics: the four equations**
Don't read first. Write all four kinematic equations cold. For every one you miss, write it three times and say out loud what each variable means. Rewrite the full set from scratch once more. Check pass at 10:40.

*Break 10:45–10:55*

**10:55–11:20 | Kinematics: projectile motion**
Write this down first as a rule: horizontal and vertical are completely independent. Then work one projectile problem treating each axis separately. Don't let them mix until finding total speed or angle at the very end. Check pass at 11:15.

*Break 11:20–11:30*

**11:30–11:50 | Forces: Newton's laws + friction**
Write all three laws in your own words first, then in formula form. Words matter — formulas alone blank under pressure when questions are phrased differently. Then drill the difference between static and kinetic friction: why μ_s > μ_k, and what "adjusts to match applied force" actually means in a problem. Check pass at 11:45.

*Stop at 11:50. Sleep.*

## Day 2
*Math: Matrices. Physics: Rotational Motion + SHM.*
*Physics is your weakest area — it gets the earlier slot tonight only.*

### PHYSICS — 8:00pm to 9:50pm

**8:00–8:25 | Rotational Motion: the linear-angular parallel**
Write the full comparison table from scratch — every row, both columns. For each angular quantity, say out loud what the linear equivalent is and why the parallel exists. The goal is to stop treating rotational motion as new content. Check pass at 8:20.

*Break 8:25–8:35*

**8:35–9:00 | Rotational Motion: inertia and torque**
Write the parallel axis theorem and explain in one sentence why mass far from the axis makes something harder to spin. Use the figure skater if it helps. Then do one torque calculation by hand — pay attention to the angle between r and F. Check pass at 8:55.

*Break 9:00–9:10*

**9:10–9:35 | SHM: the three equations**
Close notes, write x(t), v(t), a(t) from scratch. For every one you miss, write it three times. Then connect them — velocity is the derivative of displacement, acceleration is the derivative of velocity. If you see that relationship you can reconstruct any one from the others. Check pass at 9:30.

*Break 9:35–9:45*

**9:45–10:05 | SHM: energy, pendulums, damping**
Drill the K/U swap logic — at max displacement all PE, at equilibrium all KE, total always constant. Write the three pendulum period formulas cold and identify what makes each type different. For damping: just know what the exponential envelope does to amplitude and what b controls. Check pass at 10:00.

---

### MATH — 10:15pm to 11:55pm

**10:15–10:40 | Determinants**
Don't start with reading. Try writing the 3×3 cofactor expansion cold. Check what's wrong, understand why, rewrite it correctly. Write the sign pattern grid. Repeat the full expansion once more from scratch. Check pass at 10:35.

*Break 10:40–10:50*

**10:50–11:15 | Matrix inversion**
Write the 2×2 shortcut and the general adj(A)/det(A) method from memory. Do one full 2×2 inversion by hand with numbers that aren't clean — forces you to use the method properly. Check pass at 11:10.

*Break 11:15–11:25*

**11:25–11:55 | Gaussian elimination + Rank/Nullity**
Write the three allowed row operations, then work one full system [A|B] to echelon form and back-substitute without checking mid-way. After getting an answer, write rank and nullity for that matrix. Connect rank to what it tells you about solutions — write that connection in plain words, not just formulas. Check pass at 11:50.

*Stop at 11:55. Sleep.*

## Day 3
*Math: Complex Numbers + Linear Transformations. Physics: Energy + Momentum.*

### MATH — 8:00pm to 10:10pm

**8:00–8:25 | Complex Numbers: Cartesian and polar forms**
Pick one complex number in Q2 or Q3 — forces the quadrant rule. Convert it fully: Cartesian → polar → exponential. Multiply it by another in polar form and verify it matches Cartesian multiplication. If it matches, you understand the system. Check pass at 8:20.

*Break 8:25–8:35*

**8:35–9:00 | Complex Numbers: De Moivre and roots**
Write De Moivre's theorem cold, then find the cube roots of something. Draw the Argand diagram — actually draw it. The visual locks the "equally spaced around a circle" idea in a way the formula alone won't. Check pass at 8:55.

*Break 9:00–9:10*

**9:10–9:35 | Linear Transformations: definitions + rotation/reflection**
Sketch what a rotation matrix does to a unit square — draw it. Then write the rotation and reflection matrices from memory and verify det = 1 and det = −1 respectively. Visual first, formula second. Check pass at 9:30.

*Break 9:35–9:45*

**9:45–10:10 | Linear Transformations: image finding + composition**
Drill the composition order rule (right-to-left: S then T → C = TS) with one example. Then do one image-finding problem using the inverse method. Check pass at 10:05.

---

### PHYSICS — 10:20pm to 11:50pm

**10:20–10:45 | Energy: work-KE theorem + potential energy**
Write the work-KE theorem and both PE formulas from memory. Then work one problem where you have to decide which PE formula applies — that decision step is where marks are lost, not the arithmetic. Check pass at 10:40.

*Break 10:45–10:55*

**10:55–11:20 | Energy: conservation + power**
Write both versions of conservation (with and without friction) and identify what changes between them. Do one problem with friction — find thermal energy generated (f_k · d) and confirm total energy is accounted for. Power is quick — two formulas, one example, move on. Check pass at 11:15.

*Break 11:20–11:30*

**11:30–11:50 | Momentum: types + collision logic**
Write the difference between elastic and inelastic in plain English before touching formulas. What is actually lost in an inelastic collision and where does it go? Then write the elastic collision shortcuts and state the condition they require (target at rest). Verify the equal-mass case makes physical sense. Check pass at 11:45.

*Stop at 11:50. Sleep.*

## Day 4
*Math: Curvilinear Coordinates + Weak Spot + Timed Review. Physics: Thermodynamics + Kinetic Theory + Fluids.*

### MATH — 8:00pm to 10:10pm

**8:00–8:25 | Curvilinear: coordinate conversions**
Write all three conversion sets (polar, cylindrical, spherical) from memory. Spherical is the one people blank on — if you blank, check, rewrite three times, move on. Don't spiral. Check pass at 8:20.

*Break 8:25–8:35*

**8:35–9:00 | Curvilinear: curvature + parametric curves**
Work one curvature problem fully by hand. Then write the parametric velocity and acceleration formulas. One clean worked example for curvature is enough — don't overdo this section. Check pass at 8:55.

*Break 9:00–9:10*

**9:10–9:35 | Weak spot session**
Go back through Days 1–3 of math. Find the one specific thing that still feels unresolved — the exact step, not the whole topic. Spend this entire session only on that. Not a full re-read. Surgical. Check pass at 9:30.

*Break 9:35–9:45*

**9:45–10:10 | Timed review — one question per topic**
Six questions, one from each math topic, ~4 minutes each. No notes. Don't mark harshly — the goal is to see where your brain goes with no safety net. Check after all six. Note errors, don't fix them tonight. Check pass at 10:05.

---

### PHYSICS — 10:20pm to 11:50pm

**10:20–10:45 | Thermodynamics: first law + special processes**
Write the first law, then the four special process cases as a table — process name, what's zero, what that implies. This is a classic exam target. A self-made table beats re-reading every time. Check pass at 10:40.

*Break 10:45–10:55*

**10:55–11:20 | Thermodynamics + Kinetic Theory: heat, phase changes, gas law**
Write the Kelvin rule first — underline it. Then Q = mcΔT vs Q = Lm — know which applies when and why temperature stays flat during phase change. Then write both ideal gas law forms (moles and molecules) and the work done formulas for all three special volume cases. Check pass at 11:15.

*Break 11:20–11:30*

**11:30–11:50 | Fluids: pressure, Archimedes, Bernoulli**
For each concept write the formula, then write one real-world example next to it. The example is not decoration — it's the memory hook. Bernoulli explaining airplane lift is more memorable than the equation alone. Continuity last: narrower pipe → faster flow, same volume per second. Check pass at 11:45.

*Stop at 11:50. Sleep.*
