### 8:00–8:25 | Curvilinear: coordinate conversions

#### READ THIS FIRST

Three coordinate systems. Write all three conversion sets. Spherical is the one people blank on.

**2D Polar $(x, y) \leftrightarrow (r, \theta)$:**

$$x = r\cos\theta, \quad y = r\sin\theta$$

$$r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}, \quad \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right) \text{ — check quadrant}$$

**Cylindrical $(x, y, z) \leftrightarrow (r, \theta, z)$:**

$$x = r\cos\theta, \quad y = r\sin\theta, \quad z = z$$

$$r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}, \quad \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right), \quad z = z$$

Cylindrical is just polar with $z$ left unchanged.

**Spherical $(x, y, z) \leftrightarrow (\rho, \theta, \phi)$:**

Convention used here: $\rho$ = distance from origin, $\theta$ = polar angle from $z$-axis (0 to $\pi$), $\phi$ = azimuthal angle in $xy$-plane (0 to $2\pi$).

$$x = \rho\sin\theta\cos\phi$$

$$y = \rho\sin\theta\sin\phi$$

$$z = \rho\cos\theta$$

$$\rho = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}, \quad \theta = \arccos\!\left(\frac{z}{\rho}\right), \quad \phi = \arctan\!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)$$

**Why $x$ and $y$ both have $\sin\theta$ out front:** You first tilt down from the $z$-axis by $\theta$. The projection onto the $xy$-plane has length $\rho\sin\theta$. Then $\cos\phi$ and $\sin\phi$ split that projection into $x$ and $y$. The $z$ component is simply $\rho\cos\theta$ — the projection directly onto the $z$-axis.

**Geometric meaning — say this aloud:**
- $\rho$ = how far from the origin
- $\theta$ = angle down from the north pole ($z$-axis)
- $\phi$ = which direction around the equator

**Note:** Some courses swap $\theta$ and $\phi$. Match your lecturer's notation — the structure is what matters.

### 8:35–9:00 | Curvilinear: curvature + parametric curves

#### READ THIS FIRST

**Parametric curve** $\mathbf{r}(t) = [x(t),\ y(t),\ z(t)]$:

$$\mathbf{r}'(t) = [x'(t),\ y'(t),\ z'(t)] \quad \text{[velocity vector]}$$

$$|\mathbf{r}'(t)| = \sqrt{x'^2 + y'^2 + z'^2} \quad \text{[speed]}$$

$$\mathbf{r}''(t) = [x''(t),\ y''(t),\ z''(t)] \quad \text{[acceleration vector]}$$

**Arc length:**

$$L = \int_a^b |\mathbf{r}'(t)|\ dt$$

**Curvature $\kappa$:**

$$\kappa = \frac{|\mathbf{r}'(t) \times \mathbf{r}''(t)|}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|^3}$$

For a 2D curve $y = f(x)$:

$$\kappa = \frac{|y''|}{(1 + y'^2)^{3/2}}$$

**What curvature means:** How sharply the curve bends. $\kappa = 0$ for a straight line. $\kappa = 1/R$ for a circle of radius $R$. High $\kappa$ = tight bend.

**Unit tangent vector:**

$$\hat{T}(t) = \frac{\mathbf{r}'(t)}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|}$$

**Worked example — curvature of a parabola:**

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = [t,\ t^2,\ 0]$$

$$\mathbf{r}'(t) = [1,\ 2t,\ 0], \quad \mathbf{r}''(t) = [0,\ 2,\ 0]$$

$$\mathbf{r}' \times \mathbf{r}'' = \begin{vmatrix}\hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ 1 & 2t & 0 \\ 0 & 2 & 0\end{vmatrix} = [0,\ 0,\ 2]$$

$$|\mathbf{r}' \times \mathbf{r}''| = 2, \quad |\mathbf{r}'|^3 = (1 + 4t^2)^{3/2}$$

$$\kappa = \frac{2}{(1 + 4t^2)^{3/2}}$$

At $t = 0$: $\kappa = 2$ — maximum curvature at the vertex of the parabola.

### 10:20–10:45 | Thermodynamics: first law + special processes

#### READ THIS FIRST

**First law of thermodynamics:**

$$\Delta U = Q - W$$

- $\Delta U$ = change in internal energy of the system
- $Q$ = heat added **to** the system
- $W$ = work done **by** the system

**Sign convention — write this out every time until it is automatic:**

| Quantity | Positive when | Negative when |
|----------|---------------|---------------|
| $Q$ | Heat flows into system | Heat flows out of system |
| $W$ | System expands (does work on surroundings) | System is compressed (surroundings do work on it) |

**Work done by a gas:**

$$W = \int p\ dV$$

For constant pressure (isobaric): $W = p\Delta V$.

**The four special processes — build this table yourself:**

| Process | Condition | What is zero | Simplified first law |
|---------|-----------|--------------|----------------------|
| Isothermal | $T = \text{const}$ | $\Delta U = 0$ | $Q = W$ |
| Adiabatic | $Q = 0$ | $Q$ | $\Delta U = -W$ |
| Isochoric | $V = \text{const}$ | $W = 0$ | $\Delta U = Q$ |
| Isobaric | $p = \text{const}$ | nothing zero | $\Delta U = Q - p\Delta V$ |

The exam asks: given a process type, what is zero? What does that simplify the first law to? This table is the answer.

#### NOW: Close the file. Write $\Delta U = Q - W$, the sign convention table, and the four special processes table from memory.

### 10:55–11:20 | Thermodynamics + Kinetic Theory: heat, phase changes, ideal gas

#### READ THIS FIRST

**Kelvin rule — underline this:**

$$T(\text{K}) = T(°\text{C}) + 273.15$$

All gas law calculations require Kelvin. Using Celsius gives wrong answers. Convert first, every time.

**Two heat formulas — know when each applies:**

$$Q = mc\Delta T \quad \text{[temperature is changing]}$$

$$Q = Lm \quad \text{[temperature is NOT changing — phase transition in progress]}$$

**Why temperature flatlines during a phase change:** The energy input goes into breaking intermolecular bonds, not into increasing molecular kinetic energy. Temperature only rises again once the phase change is complete.

Types of latent heat:
- $L_f$ = latent heat of fusion (melting or freezing)
- $L_v$ = latent heat of vaporisation (boiling or condensing)

**Ideal gas law — two equivalent forms:**

$$pV = nRT \quad (R = 8.314\ \text{J/mol·K},\ n = \text{moles})$$

$$pV = Nk_BT \quad (k_B = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}\ \text{J/K},\ N = \text{number of molecules})$$

Relationship: $R = N_A k_B$.

**Work done by gas in each process:**

| Process | Work $W$ |
|---------|---------|
| Isothermal | $W = nRT\ln\!\left(\dfrac{V_f}{V_i}\right)$ |
| Isobaric | $W = p\Delta V$ |
| Isochoric | $W = 0$ |
| Adiabatic | $W = -\Delta U = nC_V(T_i - T_f)$ |

#### NOW: Close the file. Write the Kelvin conversion, both heat formulas with their conditions, both ideal gas law forms, and the work formula table.

Reopen and check.

### 11:30–11:50 | Kinetic Theory: internal energy, specific heats, RMS speed

#### READ THIS FIRST

**Internal energy:**

$$E_{\text{int}} = \frac{f}{2}nRT$$

where $f$ = degrees of freedom:
- Monoatomic ideal gas (He, Ne, Ar): $f = 3 \implies E_{\text{int}} = \frac{3}{2}nRT$
- Diatomic ideal gas (N₂, O₂): $f = 5$ at room temperature $\implies E_{\text{int}} = \frac{5}{2}nRT$

**Physical meaning:** Internal energy is the total random kinetic energy of all molecules. It depends only on temperature — not pressure or volume.

**Molar specific heats:**

$$C_V = \frac{f}{2}R \quad \text{[constant volume]}$$

$$C_p = C_V + R \quad \text{[constant pressure — always, for any ideal gas]}$$

$$\gamma = \frac{C_p}{C_V}$$

**Values to know cold:**

| Gas type | $C_V$ | $C_p$ | $\gamma$ |
|----------|-------|-------|----------|
| Monoatomic | $\frac{3}{2}R$ | $\frac{5}{2}R$ | $\frac{5}{3}$ |
| Diatomic | $\frac{5}{2}R$ | $\frac{7}{2}R$ | $\frac{7}{5}$ |

**Why $C_p > C_V$:** At constant pressure, expanding gas does work $p\Delta V$ on the surroundings. That energy must also come from the heat input, on top of the energy needed to raise temperature. At constant volume, all heat goes directly into $\Delta U$.

**RMS speed:**

$$v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3RT}{M}} \quad \text{($M$ = molar mass in kg/mol)}$$

Or per molecule: $v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3k_BT}{m}}$.

Why lighter gases move faster: $M$ is in the denominator. Smaller molar mass → higher $v_{\text{rms}}$ at the same $T$.

**Adiabatic relations — two forms:**

$$pV^\gamma = \text{constant} \quad \text{[use when given } p \text{ and } V\text{]}$$

$$TV^{\gamma-1} = \text{constant} \quad \text{[use when given } T \text{ and } V\text{]}$$

$\gamma = 5/3$ for monoatomic, $\gamma = 7/5$ for diatomic.
