### 8:00–8:25 | Rotational Motion: the linear-angular parallel

#### READ THIS FIRST

Rotational motion is not new content. Every rotational quantity has a direct linear equivalent. The equations are structurally identical — only the symbols change. If you already know $F = ma$, you already know $\tau = I\alpha$.

The one equation that connects both systems:

$$v = r\omega$$

Tangential speed at radius $r$ from an axis spinning at $\omega$ rad/s. If you are 2 m from the axis and it spins at 3 rad/s, you move at 6 m/s tangentially.

The full parallel table:

| Linear | Angular |
|--------|---------|
| $x$ — displacement (m) | $\theta$ — angular displacement (rad) |
| $v = \frac{dx}{dt}$ | $\omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt}$ |
| $a = \frac{dv}{dt}$ | $\alpha = \frac{d\omega}{dt}$ |
| $F = ma$ | $\tau = I\alpha$ |
| $p = mv$ | $L = I\omega$ |
| $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ | $KE = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$ |
| $W = Fd$ | $W = \tau\theta$ |

Say this aloud after reading: *"Every rotational equation is the linear version with $m \to I$, $v \to \omega$, $F \to \tau$. I already know this system."*

### 8:35–9:00 | Rotational Motion: moment of inertia + torque

#### READ THIS FIRST

**Moment of inertia** $I$ is the rotational equivalent of mass — it measures resistance to angular acceleration. It depends on how mass is distributed relative to the axis, not just the total mass.

Common shapes — these are what exam questions use:

$$I_{\text{solid disk or cylinder, about centre}} = \frac{1}{2}MR^2$$

$$I_{\text{hoop or ring, about centre}} = MR^2$$

$$I_{\text{solid sphere}} = \frac{2}{5}MR^2$$

$$I_{\text{rod about its centre}} = \frac{1}{12}ML^2$$

$$I_{\text{rod about its end}} = \frac{1}{3}ML^2$$

**Parallel axis theorem** — when the axis is not through the centre of mass:

$$I = I_{cm} + Md^2$$

$d$ = distance from centre of mass to the new axis. Because $d$ is squared, doubling the distance from the axis quadruples rotational inertia. This is why a figure skater pulling arms in (reducing $d$) causes $\omega$ to increase — $L = I\omega$ is conserved, $I$ drops, so $\omega$ must rise.

**Torque:**

$$\tau = rF\sin\theta$$

$\theta$ is the angle **between** $\vec{r}$ (the position vector from the pivot to where force is applied) and $\vec{F}$ (the force vector). This is the angle that trips people up — it is not the angle between $\vec{F}$ and the horizontal.

- $\theta = 90°$: maximum torque (force perpendicular to lever arm)
- $\theta = 0°$ or $180°$: zero torque (force acts along or directly against the arm, through the pivot)

**Worked example:**
Wrench, length $r = 0.5\ \text{m}$. Force $F = 20\ \text{N}$ applied at $30°$ to the handle.

$$\tau = (0.5)(20)\sin 30° = (0.5)(20)(0.5) = 5\ \text{N·m}$$

### 9:10–9:35 | SHM: the three equations

#### READ THIS FIRST

Simple harmonic motion has three equations of motion. They are derivatives of each other — understanding this relationship lets you reconstruct any one from the others if you blank in an exam.

$$x(t) = x_m \cos(\omega t + \phi) \quad \text{[displacement]}$$

$$v(t) = -\omega x_m \sin(\omega t + \phi) \quad \text{[velocity — derivative of } x \text{]}$$

$$a(t) = -\omega^2 x_m \cos(\omega t + \phi) \quad \text{[acceleration — derivative of } v \text{]}$$

The relationship that defines SHM:

$$\boxed{a(t) = -\omega^2 x(t)}$$

Acceleration is always proportional to displacement and always opposite in sign. If you see this in a problem, it is SHM by definition.

**What each symbol means:**
- $x_m$ = amplitude — maximum displacement from equilibrium, always positive
- $\omega$ = angular frequency in rad/s — **not** the same as frequency $f$
- $\phi$ = phase constant — determined by the initial conditions at $t = 0$

**Connecting frequency measures:**

$$\omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T}$$

**Most common exam trap:** A question gives $f$ in Hz but the formula needs $\omega$. Always convert first: $\omega = 2\pi f$.

### 9:45–10:05 | SHM: energy, pendulums, damping

#### READ THIS FIRST

**Energy in SHM:**

$$PE = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 \qquad KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

$$E_{\text{total}} = \frac{1}{2}kx_m^2 = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x_m^2 \quad \text{(constant — conserved throughout motion)}$$

**The energy swap — learn this pattern:**

| Position | KE | PE |
|----------|----|----|
| $x = \pm x_m$ (max displacement) | 0 | Maximum — all energy is PE |
| $x = 0$ (equilibrium) | Maximum — all energy is KE | 0 |
| Anywhere between | Both exist | Both exist |

At equilibrium, $v = \pm\omega x_m$ (maximum speed).

**Three pendulum types and their periods:**

$$T_{\text{simple}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} \quad \text{[only depends on length and gravity]}$$

$$T_{\text{physical}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{I}{mgd}} \quad \text{[$d$ = distance from pivot to centre of mass]}$$

$$T_{\text{torsional}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{I}{\kappa}} \quad \text{[$\kappa$ = torsional constant — no gravity term]}$$

Why they differ: Simple uses geometry only. Physical accounts for actual mass distribution via $I$. Torsional is gravity-independent — the restoring force is the twisted spring constant $\kappa$.

**Damping:**

The amplitude decays according to:

$$x_m(t) = x_m \cdot e^{-\frac{b}{2m}t}$$

- $b$ = damping constant. Larger $b$ = faster decay.
- **Underdamped:** oscillates with shrinking amplitude
- **Critically damped:** returns to zero fastest, no oscillation
- **Overdamped:** returns slowly, no oscillation

Exams ask about the envelope $e^{-bt/2m}$ — not the shifted frequency. Know what $b$ controls.

#### NOW: Close the file. Write the energy formulas, the swap table, all three pendulum formulas, and the damping envelope from memory.

Reopen and check.

### 10:15–10:40 | Determinants

#### READ THIS FIRST — then cold-write (you have prior exposure here)

**2×2 determinant:**

$$\det\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} = ad - bc$$

**3×3 cofactor expansion along row 1:**

For $A = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \\ c_1 & c_2 & c_3 \end{pmatrix}$:

$$\det(A) = a_1(b_2c_3 - b_3c_2) - a_2(b_1c_3 - b_3c_1) + a_3(b_1c_2 - b_2c_1)$$

Write this sign grid before every 3×3 expansion — it eliminates sign errors:

$$\begin{pmatrix} + & - & + \\ - & + & - \\ + & - & + \end{pmatrix} \quad C_{ij} = (-1)^{i+j}$$

The middle term in a row 1 expansion is **always negative**. This is where most errors happen.

**Sarrus' rule (3×3 only):** Copy columns 1 and 2 to the right of the matrix. Three down-right diagonals → positive. Three down-left diagonals → negative. Sum them.

**Key properties:**

| Operation | Effect on det |
|-----------|---------------|
| Swap two rows | Sign flips |
| Row of zeros | $\det = 0$ |
| One row = multiple of another | $\det = 0$ |
| Multiply one row by $k$ | $\det$ multiplies by $k$ |
| $\det(AB)$ | $= \det(A)\cdot\det(B)$ |
| $\det(A^T)$ | $= \det(A)$ |

#### NOW: Close the file. Write the 2×2 formula, the 3×3 expansion, the sign grid, and all properties cold.

Reopen and check.

### 10:50–11:15 | Matrix inversion

#### READ THIS FIRST — then cold-write

**Check this first:** $A^{-1}$ only exists if $\det(A) \neq 0$. If $\det(A) = 0$, the matrix is **singular** — no inverse exists. State this and stop.

**2×2 shortcut:**

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \implies A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det(A)}\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$$

The pattern: swap the main diagonal entries, negate the off-diagonal entries, divide everything by $\det(A)$.

**General method — $\text{adj}(A) / \det(A)$:**

1. Compute $\det(A)$
2. Build cofactor matrix $C$: each entry $C_{ij} = (-1)^{i+j} \cdot M_{ij}$, where $M_{ij}$ is the determinant of the submatrix with row $i$ and column $j$ removed
3. Transpose $C$ to get $\text{adj}(A) = C^T$
4. $A^{-1} = \dfrac{\text{adj}(A)}{\det(A)}$

**Always verify:** Compute $A \cdot A^{-1}$. If it does not equal $I$, find the exact line where it broke.

**Worked example:**

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 5 & 2 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \det(A) = (3)(2)-(1)(5) = 1$$

$$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{1}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -5 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\text{Verify: } \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 5 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -5 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \checkmark$$

### 11:25–11:55 | Gaussian elimination + Rank/Nullity

#### READ THIS FIRST — then cold-write

**The three row operations:**

1. $R_i \leftrightarrow R_j$ — swap any two rows
2. $R_i \to kR_i$ — multiply a row by a non-zero scalar
3. $R_i \to R_i + kR_j$ — add a multiple of one row to another

Label every operation as you go. $R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1$. This prevents losing track and shows working to the examiner.

**Row echelon form (REF):**
- All zero rows at the bottom
- Each leading entry (pivot) is strictly to the right of the pivot in the row above

**Reduced row echelon form (RREF):** Same as REF, plus all pivots are 1 and all other entries in pivot columns are 0.

**Process:** Write $[A|b]$, row reduce to echelon form, then back-substitute.

**Rank and Nullity:**

$$\text{rank}(A) = \text{number of pivot columns in echelon form}$$

$$\text{nullity}(A) = n - \text{rank}(A), \quad n = \text{number of columns of } A$$

$$\text{rank}(A) + \text{nullity}(A) = n \quad \text{[Rank-Nullity Theorem]}$$

**What rank tells you about solutions:**

| Condition | Number of solutions |
|-----------|-------------------|
| $\text{rank}(A) = \text{rank}([A\|b])$ and equals $n$ | Unique solution |
| $\text{rank}(A) = \text{rank}([A\|b])$ and is less than $n$ | Infinitely many (free variables) |
| $\text{rank}(A) \neq \text{rank}([A\|b])$ | No solution — inconsistent |

In plain words: *"If augmenting the matrix creates a new pivot that didn't exist in $A$ alone, the system is contradictory. If there are fewer pivots than unknowns, there are free variables and infinitely many solutions."*
