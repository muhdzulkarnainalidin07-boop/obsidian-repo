**Physics**
## Day 1
*Math: [[Vectors]] + [[Lines_and_Planes]]. Physics: [[Kinematics]] + [[Forces]].*

### MATH — 8:00pm to 10:10pm

**8:00–8:25 | Vectors: basics and dot product**
Read your Vectors notes for 10 minutes, then close them. Spend the remaining 15 minutes writing out every dot product formula and rule from memory. Don't check until the check pass at 8:20.

*Break 8:25–8:35*

**8:35–9:00 | Vectors: cross product**
Close notes immediately. Reconstruct the cross product determinant expansion from scratch. Write it out 3 times if needed — this one needs motor memory. Check pass at 8:55.

*Break 9:00–9:10*

**9:10–9:35 | Lines & Planes: line forms**
Read the three line forms once, then close. Write all three from memory with a made-up example for each. Feel the difference between them, don't just copy the formula. Check pass at 9:30.

*Break 9:35–9:45*

**9:45–10:10 | Lines & Planes: planes and intersections**
Focus on finding the normal vector from three points — that step trips most people. Write the plane equation, the method for getting n from three points, and all distance/angle formulas from memory. Check pass at 10:05.

---

### PHYSICS — 10:20pm to 11:50pm

**10:20–10:45 | Kinematics: the four equations**
Don't read first. Write all four kinematic equations cold. For every one you miss, write it three times and say out loud what each variable means. Rewrite the full set from scratch once more. Check pass at 10:40.

*Break 10:45–10:55*

**10:55–11:20 | Kinematics: projectile motion**
Write this down first as a rule: horizontal and vertical are completely independent. Then work one projectile problem treating each axis separately. Don't let them mix until finding total speed or angle at the very end. Check pass at 11:15.

*Break 11:20–11:30*

**11:30–11:50 | Forces: Newton's laws + friction**
Write all three laws in your own words first, then in formula form. Words matter — formulas alone blank under pressure when questions are phrased differently. Then drill the difference between static and kinetic friction: why μ_s > μ_k, and what "adjusts to match applied force" actually means in a problem. Check pass at 11:45.

*Stop at 11:50. Sleep.*
